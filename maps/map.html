Induced earthquakes in the Netherlands as a result of gas extraction

By Stan Helsloot hallo

Gasfield

In 1959 one of the worlds largest gas reserves was discovered in Groningen, a province in the northeast of the Netherlands. Four years after its discovery, gas extraction begun. Earthquakes have since causes large amounts of structural damages.[1] To show the relation between the earthquakes and the gas extraction, this page provides several visualizations to clarify the situation.

Extraction

Over the past "two decades" gas extraction has been heavily reduced due to demages caused by induced earthquakes.[2] The current goal is to keep reducing gas extraction and to lower the total extraction to less then five billion Nm^3 in 2023.[3] Several measures are taken to speed up the reduction, such as limiting export to neighbouring countries and building a nitrogen factory which can be used to convert highly caloric types of gas to the low caloric type.

Earthquakes

Starting in 1911, the KNMI (Royal Netherlands Meteorological Institute) has been keeping a record of earthquakes occuring or affecting the Netherlands. The first earthquake caused by gas extraction was detected in Assen in 1986 and had a magnitude of 2.8 on the Richter scale.[4] After this, the amount of earthquakes has increased significantly in quantity as can be seen in figure "stacked_year". These earthquakes, refered to as induced earthquakes, differ from tectonic earthquakes mainly in the depth of occurence. Induced earthquakes have an approximate depth of 3 km, whilst naturally occuring earthquake occur between 20 and 100 km depth. In 2015 extra seismographs have been installed throughout the Netherlands, allowing for detection of earthquakes with magnitudes of below 1.5 on the Richter scale. These earthquakes were not used in the visualizations on this page.
