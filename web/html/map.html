Gasfield

In 1959 one of the worlds largest gas reserves was discovered in Groningen, a province in the northeast of the Netherlands. Four years after its discovery, gas extraction begun. Earthquakes have since causes large amounts of structural damages.[1] This page touch on all gas extracted from Dutch soil. The numbers for amount of gas extracted are rounden to billion (M) cubic meters (m^3) and are all measured at standard (N) conditions (0 degrees C and 1 atmosphere). All magnitudes of the earthquakes are on the Richter Scale. To show the relation between the earthquakes and the gas extraction, this page provides several visualizations to clarify the situation.

Extraction

Over the past "two decades" gas extraction has been heavily reduced due to demages caused by induced earthquakes.[2] The current goal is to keep reducing gas extraction and to lower the total extraction to less then five billion Nm^3 in 2023. Several measures are taken to speed up the reduction, such as limiting export to neighbouring countries and building a nitrogen factory which can be used to convert highly caloric types of gas to the low caloric type, which is used for coocking and heating appliances. To show the current trend of decreasing gas extraction, two plots have been made. On the right, a plot showing the yearly totals of gas extracted, and to the left a plot showing the total amounts of gas extracted per month. Clicking on the bars of the plot on the right will change the year of which the months are displayed.

Earthquakes

Starting in 1911, the KNMI (Royal Netherlands Meteorological Institute) has been keeping a record of earthquakes occuring or affecting the Netherlands. The first earthquake caused by gas extraction, a so called induced earthquake, was detected in Assen in 1986 and had a magnitude of 2.8 on the Richter scale.[3] After this, the amount of earthquakes per year has increased significantly in quantity as can be seen in figure to the right. These induced earthquakes differ from tectonic earthquakes mainly in the depth of occurence. Induced earthquakes have an approximate depth of 3 km, whilst naturally occuring earthquake occur between 20 and 100 km depth. In 2015 extra seismographs have been installed throughout the Netherlands, allowing for detection of earthquakes with magnitudes of below 1.5 on the Richter scale. These earthquakes were not used in the visualizations on this page. To the right is a visualization of the amount of earthquakes occuring in a certain range on the Richter scale. Clicking on one of the bars will show the selected year on the left, which is a map of the Netherlands on which the earthquakes are visualized.
